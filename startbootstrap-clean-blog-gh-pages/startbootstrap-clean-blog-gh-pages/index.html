Major Depression

A complex dynamic system characterized by depression symtoms

Major depression (MD) imposes a heavy burden on those suffering from it, as well as on the people around them. As appoximately 17% of the population gets affected by MD at least once in their life, it can be viewed as one of the biggest mental health problems of our time.

The current knowledge about MS pathogenesis is still unknown, with the most important unanswered question remaining:

'What makes some people more vulnerable to Major Depression than others?'

To try and answer this question Cramer et al. (2016) made a network model. In MD it is assumed that symptoms cause other symptoms. For example, as a people suffers from insomnia, they will experience fatigue. This may lead to the development of concentration problems and the loss of interest. These relations between the symptoms have the potential to trigger an episode of MD.

In the network below, the symptoms are represented by the nodes. The edges represent the relation between these symptoms.

The MD-network as shown below is referred to as a complex dynamic system. Complex because the relations between symptoms may lead may lead to properties of the system that are impossible to predict from the individual systems. The whole is more than the sum of the parts! The system is dynamic, because this network of symptom-relations develops over time. The MD system has two attractor states: 'depressed' and 'non-depressed'.
